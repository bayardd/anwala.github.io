    Pelosi: Democrats Will Review Options if Trump Declares National Emergency

    House Speaker Nancy Pelosi (D., Calif.) said Democrats would review their options should President Trump declare a national emergency, but held off from saying Democrats would automatically bring up a resolution of disapproval.

    "We have to see what the president actually says," she said.

    White House Press Secretary Sarah Sanders, asked Thursday afternoon if the White House was ready for legal challenges to a national emergency declaration, said, “We are very prepared but there shouldn’t be [a legal challenge]. The president is doing his job. Congress should do theirs.”

    Trump to Sign Border Bill but Also Declare National Emergency

    President Trump plans to sign the spending bill that Congress was set to pass later Thursday and will then declare a national emergency to get more funding for border barriers,Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell said.

    "He's prepared to sign the bill," Mr. McConnell said Thursday on the Senate floor, after a conversation with the president. "He will also be issuing a national emergency declaration at the same time."

    Several minutes later the White House confirmed Mr. McConnell's comments, saying the president would take “executive action—including a national emergency” to build a wall and stop what it said was a national security crisis at the border.

    The declaration of a national emergency could enable Mr. Trump to divert funds from elsewhere to fund the wall. Democrats are expected to challenge any efforts to move money around without congressional approval.

    Four Freshman House Democrats Intend to Vote Against Border-Funding Deal

    A small group liberal House Democrats plans to oppose a funding deal that would prevent a government shutdown, leaving less wiggle room for Republicans and Democrats pushing to pass the legislation.

    Reps. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez (D., N.Y), Ayanna Pressley (D., Mass.), Ilhan Omar (D., Minn.), and Rashida Tlaib (D., Mich.) said in a statement Thursday that the funding package gives too much money to immigration-enforcement authorities and that they intend to vote against it.

    “We want to be abundantly clear: this is not a rebuke of federal workers or those who depend on the services they provide, but a rejection of the hateful policies, priorities, and rhetoric of the Trump Administration,” the four freshmen lawmakers said.

    Large-scale Democratic defections could complicate the bill’s future, though some Republicans are expected to support it. Many other progressive lawmakers have indicated that they will support the bill despite their qualms about some of its provisions.

    “I will be supporting the agreement to keep government open,” Rep. Ro Khanna, a progressive Democrat from California, wrote on Twitter. “Our negotiators got a lot of our priorities funded, denied the president funding for his wall, and supported smart border security. This is the type of pragmatic approach my constituency seeks.”

    What the Border Deal Would Do

    Funding for many government agencies is set to expire at 12:01 a.m. Saturday unless Congress passes spending legislation and President Trump signs it into law. The seven-bill package being voted on Thursday would fund the government for the rest of the fiscal year, which runs through September.

    The bipartisan deal, while supplying far less than the $5.7 billion that Mr. Trump had demanded to build a border wall, gives both parties political victories. Republicans touted the 55 new miles of physical barriers that will be built in the Rio Grande Valley, as well as additional investments in border security. The bill includes funding for new equipment at ports of entry, 600 new Customs officers, opioid detection equipment and other technology.

    Contractor Pay, Violence Against Women Act Were Left Out of Spending Package

    Government contractor Yvette Hicks walks inside the Smithsonian Hirshhorn National Museum of Modern Art on her first day back at work following the partial shutdown last month.Michael A. McCoy/Reuters

    The final spending bill was released late Wednesday after lawmakers sorted out some last-minute complications. The biggest snag focused on whether to include back pay for federal contractors to make up for lost compensation during the record-long partial government shutdown that ended last month. Democratic lawmakers supported the back pay, but the White House balked at it.

    Lawmakers also debated an extension of the Violence Against Women Act, which provides funding for programs to prevent domestic violence and bolster prosecutions. House Democrats pushed instead to reintroduce later a new version of the bill that would prevent people under protective orders from possessing firearms and would bar evictions of victims of domestic violence based on the actions of abusers.

    When negotiations had wrapped up Wednesday night, lawmakers had decided not to include a reauthorization of VAWA or back pay for contractors in the bill, but could address the issues later.

    SpaceX Launch Site and Butterfly Center Are Protected in Border-Wall Deal

    Natalie Andrews

    - In this Jan. 12, 2019 file photo, a motorcyclist rides near the SpaceX prototype Starship hopper at the Boca Chica Beach site in Texas.Miguel Roberts/Associated Press

    Democratic Texas Rep. Henry Cuellar scored big wins in the border negotiations that pertain to his district, which runs along the southern border. They mark key victories for the seven-term congressman who is facing a possible challenge from the left in 2020.

    While President Trump will get 55 new miles of barriers along the border, Mr. Cuellar successfully blocked five areas from any construction.

    Mr. Cuellar cited environmental reasons for blocking any barriers around the Santa Ana Wildlife Refuge, the Bentsen-Rio Grande Valley State Park, the National Butterfly Center, La Lomita Historial Park, where an old church sits near the border. He also successfully blocked building barriers where SpaceX has a launch site.

    The areas are deeply important to his constituents and he said several times throughout the negotiations that he would support barriers along the border -- but wanted to avoid those key sites.

    Justice Democrats, a political committee launched after the 2016 election, is trying to find a challenger to Mr. Cuellar, who is a centrist "Blue Dog" and one of the few Democrats in Congress who opposes abortion rights.

    U.S. Representative Henry Cuellar (D., Texas) speaks about border wall policy as U.S. President Donald Trump holds a bipartisan meeting with legislators last month.Jonathan Ernst/Reuters

    Senate Is Expected to Vote First, Followed by the House

    The Senate is expected to vote first Thursday afternoon, followed by the House Thursday evening, on the $333 billion package of seven spending bills. The agreement includes controversial funding for the Homeland Security Department, which oversees the border with Mexico, and whose budget triggered a five-week shutdown that ended last month.

    While liberal and conservative lawmakers have expressed opposition to elements of the package, the agreement is expected to pass both chambers with a coalition of pragmatic lawmakers eager to avoid another punishing shutdown. Mr. Trump has expressed misgivings about the level of border-wall funding, but people familiar with his plans said he plans to sign the bills.

    “We cannot repeat the disastrous government shutdown, so it is incumbent on Congress to come together to responsibly fund our government,” said House Appropriations Committee Chairwoman Nita Lowey (D., N.Y.), who led the 17-lawmaker group that struck the agreement. “This legislation represents a bipartisan compromise and will keep our government open while funding key priorities.”

    The Border Wall, Mile by Mile

    Louise Radnofsky

    The border-security deal being voted on by lawmakers would extend by about 55 miles the barriers along the U.S.’s southern border, marking a modest expansion rather than the transformative overhaul President Trump has at times described.

    At the same time, the extension could take the total length of border barriers beyond 700 miles, because of the amount of fencing and other barriers that were erected by previous administrations, and that could fulfill a goal that Mr. Trump had set for himself in the past.

    About 654 miles of the 1,991-mile border already had some kind of fabricated barrier when Mr. Trump took office. Much of the rest has natural barriers; approximately 1,295 miles of the border are river, and 696 miles are land.

    Democrats Willing to Waive Rule on 72-Hour Review of Legislation

    When they took over the House this year, Democrats approved a rule stipulating that lawmakers must have 72 hours to review legislation before they vote on it.

    But like many a January resolution, it is becoming a bit more relaxed in February.

    House Democratic leaders said Wednesday that they hoped to finalize legislation later in the day funding the government through September, including controversial border-security funds, before voting on it Thursday. That could potentially mean less than 24 hours for lawmakers to read the sweeping deal.

    “After surviving a 35-day government shutdown that put 800,000 federal workers, plus their families, plus the American people, plus the economy in harm’s way -- the right thing to do at this moment is to come together and consider this bipartisan agreement,” House Democratic Caucus Chairman Hakeem Jeffries (D., N.Y.) told reporters.

    Democrats said the new rule took situations like this into account with a waiver for emergencies. When Republicans controlled the House, they routinely waived their own three-day rule in similar scenarios, particularly with spending deals aimed at avoiding lapses in government funding.
